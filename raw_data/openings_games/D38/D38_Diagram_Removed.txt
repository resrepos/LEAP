White, on the other hand, can bring out his B before playing e3, in this way: 2. c4 e6 3. Nc3 Nf6 4. Bg5 and the game might proceed as follows: 4. ... Nd7. (Diagram 38.) No fault can be found with this move, although it blocks the Bishop, since the latter can only be developed effectively at c2. Moreover, the Knight at d7 supports the projected c5. White cannot win a pawn now with 5. cxd5 exd5 6. Nxd5 because of Nxd5 7. Bxd8 Bb4+. Therefore 5. e3 must be played first, and after Be2 6. Nf3 O-O 7. Rc1 b6 8. cxd5 exd5 9. Bd3 Bb7 all the pieces have found rational development. Quite a different system of opening ensues, when Black does not delay pushing the P to c5 until after his pieces are developed, but makes the advance on his third move.
